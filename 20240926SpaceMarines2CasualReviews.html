Warhammer 40k: Space Marines 2 Casual's Review

Posted on September 26, 2024


Today, I’m giving a Casual’s review of Space Marine 2 by Sabre Interactive. As someone with almost no experience in the Warhammer 40k universe, I feel like this game will be the entry point for a lot of people. Why? Why, this of all games? Have you seen this game?


My initial reaction was, "This looks too good to be true." Honestly, I expected it to be cancelled or fade into the aether, but it arrived—exactly as promised. I bought it for the base PlayStation 5 and played on Performance Mode. With a price tag of €70, the question is—was it worth it?

My first run was a co-op playthrough with @nopurposeindie. The game lets you team up with two other players to tackle the campaign or the multiplayer-focused mode, Operations. I'm not much into PvP, so I’ll leave those insights to the cut-throats. However, Operations mode exceeded my expectations. It feels like a separate campaign but is actually a supplement to the main storyline.

The main character interacts with other squads on the barge or via comms, but in Operations, you are those other squads. It reminded me of Mass Effect 3’s multiplayer, where you feel as though you had an impact on the overall narrative through your involvement in the multiplayer.

Thankfully, the game offers bots in Operations, so if you can’t find other players, you’re not left stranded. I always appreciated future proofing for modes like these when they inevitably go offline. The only downside is that you can’t control who joins your game unless you start with a premade team or play offline.


When it comes to presentation, Space Marine 2 absolutely excels. Scale and intensity are its biggest strengths. The characters are massive, heavy, and move with a sense of purpose. Citizens in the game constantly bow to the Space Marines, revering them as if they were deities come to bring salvation in their most dire moment.

Similarly, The Emperor’s name is invoked by everyone, from high-ranking officials to ordinary people. This not only sells the scale of the universe but also adds gravitas to the story. As someone unfamiliar with the lore, I’m deeply curious to learn more about this Emperor, the Space Marines, the Chaplins…. my wallet may be in danger here.

Their dialogue projects pride, reverence, and duty, layers masking their humanity until it shines through when the need arises resulting in some very satisfying character moments. This isn’t easy to pull off—it would’ve been all too simple to reduce these characters to "hoo-rah" meatheads. But the story wisely keeps its stakes high and its characters focused on what should be an impossible task.


The voice cast does an incredible job bringing these characters to life. Backed by an intense, often oppressive soundtrack and intricate environmental design, the game draws you in—right until the disconnections start. Sadly, they happen frequently. Most issues occurred on the battle barge, which serves as your hub between missions. We could get through the missions with only a few disconnections, but moving between them on the barge? We were booted three or four times just moving from mission end to mission start.

Thanks to previews, we knew about these problems going in and were prepared to tough it out. Still, it put a damper on the co-op experience, as we couldn’t just hang out and explore the barge at our own pace.


As far as gameplay is concerned, I loved every second of it, especially the focus on melee combat and health/armour management. Like Doom (2016) and Outriders (2021), Space Marine 2 rewards aggressive playstyles. Enemies swarm you, tearing through your health and armour, and the only way to recover is by executing stunned foes or firing follow-up shots. The game’s parryable and dodgeable attacks add to the intensity, creating heroic moments—whether you’re dodging at the last second or countering their attempts at flanking.

The only negatives I have is that the weapon variety is pretty minimal. Feels like we are swapping firing modes more so than swapping weapons. Additionally I would have liked more options earlier. I was disappointed to discover that I couldn’t equip the shield at any point in the campaign.


There are so many standout cinematic moments and intense character interactions, but they’re best experienced firsthand.

In the end, I’d recommend this game to anyone—whether you're a die-hard fan of the franchise or, like me, taking your first steps into the Warhammer 40k universe. However, if you are a multiplayer enthusiast you might need to wait a few patches for the best experience, otherwise this is a must-play.
